What Lot Can You Afford?

Understanding true lot costs and budgeting for land in your custom home project

The Purchase Price Is Just the Beginning

Someone shows us a lot listing: "Look, this property is only $50,000! That leaves more budget for the house." Maybe. Or maybe that $50,000 lot will require $30,000 in site work, utilities, and improvements before you can even think about building. Meanwhile, a $75,000 lot down the road might be build-ready with minimal additional costs.

The question isn't "what lot can I afford to buy"—it's "what lot can I afford to build on?" Those are very different questions. The true cost of a lot includes not just the purchase price, but all the expenses required to make it ready for your home.

We'll help you understand how to budget for land realistically, what factors affect lot affordability, and how to avoid expensive surprises.

Rule of Thumb: Lot Cost vs. Total Budget

As a general guideline, your lot cost (purchase price plus site improvements) should represent roughly 20-30% of your total project budget.

Example 1: If your total budget is $400,000, you might allocate $80,000-$120,000 for land.

Example 2: If your total budget is $600,000, you might spend $120,000-$180,000 on your lot.

These are guidelines, not rules. In expensive markets, lot costs might be higher. In rural areas with challenging sites, you might spend less on purchase but more on improvements. The key is balancing land costs against what you'll have left for construction.

Don't Get Land-Rich and House-Poor

We've seen people spend 40-50% of their budget on land, leaving insufficient funds to build the house they envisioned. A spectacular 10-acre lot doesn't help if you can only afford to build a small, basic home on it. Be realistic about your priorities.

What Drives Lot Prices

Understanding why lots cost what they do helps you make informed decisions.

Location and Proximity

Same as any real estate: location matters most. Lots closer to town, schools, shopping, and employment centers cost more. Rural lots farther from amenities typically cost less, but you'll drive more and might face higher utility costs.

Within the same area, specific roads or neighborhoods command premiums based on reputation, aesthetics, and proximity to desirable features.

Lot Size

Larger lots generally cost more than smaller ones, but not always proportionally. A 2-acre lot might not cost twice as much as a 1-acre lot in the same area.

Consider what size you actually need. If you want privacy and space, you need acreage. If you just want a yard for the kids and minimal maintenance, a smaller lot might serve you better and cost less.

Utilities and Infrastructure

Lots with existing utilities—public water, sewer, electricity, natural gas—command premiums because you're not paying to bring these services in.

Raw land requiring well, septic, and extended utility runs costs less upfront but more to develop. Factor these costs into your total lot budget.

Topography and Buildability

Flat, easily buildable lots cost more than steep, challenging sites. This makes sense—the flat lot requires less site work to develop.

Steep or difficult lots sell for less but can require expensive grading, retaining walls, specialized foundations, and longer driveways. Sometimes the "expensive" flat lot is actually more affordable once you account for development costs.

Views and Natural Features

Water views, mountain vistas, wooded settings—desirable features increase lot prices. Sometimes significantly. Determine what matters most to you and whether the premium is worth it.

Restrictions and Regulations

Lots in planned developments or HOA neighborhoods might cost more but come with established infrastructure, covenants, and community amenities.

Unrestricted rural land costs less but offers maximum flexibility. There's no right answer—it depends on your preferences and priorities.

Hidden Costs to Consider

Beyond the purchase price, budget for these additional lot-related expenses:

Site Work and Clearing

Heavily wooded lots might require $5,000-$15,000+ in clearing, depending on acreage and tree density. Steep slopes might need extensive grading, retaining walls, or fill—potentially $10,000-$50,000 or more for challenging sites.

Get cost estimates for site work before committing to a lot. What seems like a bargain can become expensive quickly.

Driveway Installation

Driveway costs depend on length, slope, and materials. A simple 100-foot gravel driveway might cost $3,000-$5,000. A 300-foot paved driveway up a slope could cost $15,000-$30,000 or more.

Long or steep driveways also require ongoing maintenance—plowing in winter, pothole repairs, erosion control.

Well and Septic Systems

If public utilities aren't available, budget for:

  • Well drilling: $8,000-$15,000+ depending on depth and yield
  • Septic system: $10,000-$25,000+ depending on soil conditions, system type, and site constraints
  • Perc tests and soil evaluations: $500-$2,000

Factor these into your lot budget. A $40,000 lot requiring well and septic is really a $60,000-$80,000 lot once developed.

Utility Connections

Even when public utilities exist, you pay to connect to them.

  • Electric service: $1,500-$10,000+ depending on distance from existing lines
  • Water and sewer taps: $2,000-$10,000+ depending on municipality and distance
  • Natural gas: Varies widely; sometimes available at modest cost, sometimes prohibitively expensive

Contact utility providers before purchasing to understand connection costs and timelines.

Surveys and Engineering

Budget for professional services:

  • Boundary survey: $500-$2,000
  • Topographic survey: $1,500-$5,000 for complex sites
  • Soil tests and geotechnical analysis: $1,000-$3,000
  • Septic design: $1,500-$3,000

These costs add up, but they're necessary to build safely and legally.

Carrying Costs

If you buy land before you're ready to build, you'll pay property taxes and possibly loan interest while you wait. For a $75,000 lot, annual taxes might be $1,000-$2,000 depending on location.

If you finance the land separately, interest costs add up during the construction period.

Financing Your Lot Purchase

How you pay for land affects affordability and cash flow.

Cash Purchase

Pros: No loan costs, no monthly payment, stronger negotiating position, simpler construction loan process.

Cons: Ties up capital that might be better used for construction, might limit your lot options if cash is tight.

Land Loan

Pros: Preserves cash for construction, allows you to buy land sooner.

Cons: Higher interest rates than construction loans (often 1-3% higher), shorter terms (5-15 years typical), monthly payments before you even start building, affects debt-to-income ratio for construction financing.

Many lenders will roll land loans into construction financing, but it adds complexity.

Owner Financing

Some land sellers offer financing directly. Terms vary widely—could be favorable or expensive. Have an attorney review any owner-financing agreements.

Construction-to-Permanent Loan

Some lenders offer construction loans that include land purchase. You close once, fund land and construction together, convert to permanent mortgage after completion.

Pros: Streamlined process, one closing, competitive rates.

Cons: Requires finding lot and finalizing house plans within the lender's timeline.

Evaluating Lot Value

How do you know if a lot is priced fairly?

Comparable Sales

What have similar lots in the same area sold for recently? Check county records, talk to real estate agents, ask local builders. This gives you a baseline.

Cost Per Acre (With Caution)

In rural areas, people often quote price per acre. This can be misleading—a 10-acre lot with 2 buildable acres and 8 acres of wetland isn't the same as 10 clear, buildable acres. Consider usability, not just total acreage.

Development Potential

Could the lot be subdivided in the future? Does it have commercial potential? Is the area growing? These factors affect long-term value, though they shouldn't override your immediate needs.

True Build-Ready Cost

Add up purchase price plus all development costs. Compare this total to other lots that are closer to build-ready. Sometimes paying more for a prepared lot is actually more affordable.

When to Stretch Your Lot Budget (and When Not To)

Sometimes spending more on land makes sense:

Good Reasons to Spend More on Land

Location you'll never leave: If you're planning to stay 20+ years, paying extra for your ideal location might be worth it.

Truly unique features: Water frontage, exceptional views, or rare characteristics you can't find elsewhere.

Long-term value: Land in growing areas with limited supply tends to appreciate.

Avoiding expensive development: A more expensive lot that's build-ready might be cheaper than a bargain lot requiring extensive improvements.

Don't Stretch Your Lot Budget When

It compromises the house: If a pricier lot forces you to build something inadequate, you'll regret it daily.

You're buying maximum size: More acreage than you need doesn't add value to your daily life if it just means more maintenance.

You're chasing trends: Today's hot neighborhood might not stay hot forever.

It maxes out your budget: Leave margin for unexpected costs—they always appear.

Start With the End in Mind

The goal isn't to buy the most or least expensive lot. It's to find land that fits your total project budget while supporting the home you want to build and the lifestyle you want to live.

Work backward from your total budget. If you have $500,000 to spend on land plus construction, and you want to build a 2,500 sq ft custom home at $200/sq ft, that's $500,000 for the house. That doesn't leave much for land in this scenario—you'd need to adjust something.

Being realistic about these numbers from the start prevents disappointment and helps you find the right balance between land and house.
